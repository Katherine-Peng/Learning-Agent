### 1. Deep Dive into LLMs like ChatGPT
**Andrej Karpathy** · YouTube · 3h31m · Score: 92/100
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7xTGNNLPyMI

**Summary:** Karpathy's landmark 3.5-hour general-audience deep dive into how large language models actually work — from raw data to shipped product. Covers the full training stack: pretraining (data collection, tokenization, the transformer neural network internals, GPT-2 training walkthrough, Llama 3.1 base model inference), supervised finetuning (conversation data, hallucinations, tool use, the "LLM psychology" of knowledge and self-model), and reinforcement learning (RLHF, DeepSeek-R1's reasoning-through-RL approach, AlphaGo as a template for self-improvement). Remarkable for its clarity and for naming real systems throughout rather than speaking abstractly about "the model."

**Why it matters for Week 2:** This is the definitive Week 2 resource. If you watch one thing this week, this is it — Karpathy builds up transformer architecture from first principles, walks through the actual internals of the attention mechanism, and connects tokenization → embedding → positional encoding → transformer blocks → sampling into a single coherent mental model. It's from February 2025 but was designed to stay current; the training stack he describes is exactly how Claude, GPT-5, and Gemini 3 are built today.

### 2. Pragmatic Summit Fireside Chat on Agentic Engineering — Simon Willison
**Simon Willison** · simonwillison.net · Video + post ~45 min · Score: 83/100
https://simonwillison.net/2026/Mar/14/pragmatic-summit/

**Summary:** A comprehensive fireside chat (summarized with video) covering how developers should actually think about building with coding agents right now. Willison covers four meaty areas: the adoption stages teams go through when integrating agents, test-driven development adapted for agentic workflows (and his "Showboat" tool for manual verification), the importance of sandboxing and why it can't live inside the agent layer, and prompt injection as the security threat that deserves more attention than it gets. ### 3. Why Anthropic Thinks AI Should Have Its Own Computer — Felix Rieseberg of Claude Cowork & Claude Code Desktop
**Latent Space** · YouTube · ~1h · Score: 82/100
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZpZ7lFoWaT8

**Summary:** Felix Rieseberg (who built Claude Cowork at Anthropic) explains how this tool emerged from watching non-technical users adopt Claude Code for knowledge work — and why that observation led Anthropic toward a local-first, VM-isolated architecture. The episode covers: why execution costs have dropped to the point where teams can build and test many candidate solutions simultaneously, how the VM functions as a safety boundary that operates *outside* the agent's reasoning layer (not inside it), the portability and "skills" model for reusing agent capabilities, and Anthropic's broader vision of delegating increasingly complex knowledge work to agents running on personal machines.

**Why it matters for Week 2:** This is the applied counterpart to Karpathy's theoretical deep dive. Where Karpathy explains what LLMs are, Rieseberg shows what it looks like when you wrap one in a careful architecture and give it a computer. The VM-as-safety-boundary argument is a concrete design principle you'll use when thinking about your own M365 Copilot work — where does the model end and the trusted execution environment begin?

## 🟠 Recommended

### 4. PlugMem: Transforming Raw Agent Interactions into Reusable Knowledge
**Microsoft Research** · Research blog · ~12 min read · Score: 80/100
https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/blog/from-raw-interaction-to-reusable-knowledge-rethinking-memory-for-ai-agents/

**Summary:** Microsoft Research introduces PlugMem, a plug-and-play memory module that converts agent interaction history into structured, reusable knowledge units — distinguishing between propositional facts (what is true) and prescriptive skills (what to do). Rather than storing raw text logs, PlugMem organizes these into a structured memory graph that improves retrieval precision. It consistently outperforms both generic retrieval methods and task-specific alternatives while consuming fewer memory tokens for equivalent or better performance.

**One-liner:** A concrete architecture for the "agent memory" problem you'll encounter the moment you move from single-turn to multi-session agents — directly relevant to how M365 Copilot handles user context across sessions.

## 🃏 Wild Card

### The Most Beautiful Formula Not Enough People Understand
**3Blue1Brown (Grant Sanderson)** · YouTube · ~30 min · Score: 70/100 (cross-domain trigger)
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fsLh-NYhOoU

**Why this is the Wild Card:** This video is about pure mathematics — specifically, why spheres in higher dimensions behave in geometrically counterintuitive ways (5D spheres maximize volume; 10D spheres are almost entirely "skin"). It has no AI content. But the geometric intuition it builds is the same intuition that explains why embeddings, attention, and cosine similarity work the way they do. Every transformer is doing math in hundreds or thousands of dimensions, and most engineers have no visceral sense of what that space looks like. 3Blue1Brown's visual style makes the abstract concrete. Consider this a bonus Week 2 resource for anyone who finds "768-dimensional embedding space" to be a phrase without meaning.

**Summary:** Grant explores the volumes of hyperspheres across dimensions, building up from 1D through 5D through 10D using Archimedes' classical method adapted to higher dimensions. Along the way you get a deep sense of why high-dimensional geometry is unintuitive — knowledge that transfers directly to understanding why attention mechanisms can distinguish between semantically distant concepts even in dense embedding spaces.
